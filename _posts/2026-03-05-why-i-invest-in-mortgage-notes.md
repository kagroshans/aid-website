Most physicians think of real estate as rental properties. Rental income, tenant management, cap rates, 1031 exchanges — the usual conversation. I spent time looking at that world and kept running into the same problem: passive it is not.

I want to tell you about the other side of the transaction.

## Becoming the Bank

When someone buys a house and takes out a mortgage, there are two parties. There's the borrower, who owns the property and makes monthly payments. And there's the lender, who holds the note — the legal document representing the debt — and receives those payments.

For decades, that second role belonged exclusively to banks and institutional investors. That's changing. Individual investors can now purchase existing mortgage notes from lenders, hedge funds, and other note holders. When you buy a note, you step into the lender's position. You receive the monthly payment. You hold the security interest. You don't own the property. You don't field calls about broken dishwashers.

## Why This Fits Physicians

We work in a world of protocols and checklists. Before I buy any note, I run through a structured evaluation: borrower payment history, equity position, lien status, local market data, servicer quality. It takes about 20 minutes. Either it passes or it doesn't.

That systematized approach fits the physician mindset well. There's no intuition required. There's a process, and you apply it consistently.

There's also the question of time. I work full clinical days. I have no interest in a second job. A performing mortgage note — one where the borrower is current on payments — requires almost no ongoing attention. A servicer handles payment collection, escrow, and borrower communication. My job is to screen well on the front end and let the system run.

## Where Notes Fit in a Physician's Portfolio

I want to be direct about what notes are and aren't. They're not a replacement for the index fund foundation. I still max tax-advantaged accounts. I still hold diversified equities. Notes are the next layer — an income-producing asset that complements the foundation rather than replacing it.

The yields on performing notes generally range from 7–11% depending on the note, the borrower profile, and the market. That's real cash flow, monthly, without appreciation assumptions baked in. For a physician looking to build income that doesn't depend on RVUs, that matters.

## What You Should Know Before Looking at a Note

Not every note is worth buying. Notes can be performing (borrower current) or non-performing (borrower behind). The risk profiles are completely different. Geographic concentration matters. Lien position matters — a second lien note behind a large first is a different animal than a first-lien note with 40% equity. These are learnable distinctions, but they require a framework.

That's why I built the screening checklist. It's the same set of questions I ask before every purchase. It won't tell you whether a note is a "good investment" in the abstract — nothing will. But it will tell you whether you have enough information to make a rational decision, and where the gaps are.

## The Real-Time Disclaimer

I'm not writing this from a position of retirement or hindsight. I'm a practicing neurologist actively building a note portfolio alongside clinical work. I share what I'm doing, the reasoning behind it, and what I'm learning as I go.

If this sounds like the kind of investing you've been looking for — systematic, income-focused, genuinely passive — start with the checklist. It's free, and it'll tell you quickly whether this is worth your further attention.
